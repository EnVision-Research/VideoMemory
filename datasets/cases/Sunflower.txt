Title: "The Sunflower Balcony"

shot1: On a bright Saturday morning, Lina — 29 years old, loose white shirt, rolled-up jeans, warm smile, hair in a messy bun with a pencil stuck through it — carries a small potted sunflower onto her balcony like it’s precious, sets it on the railing, and quietly says, “He’s back today.”

shot2: She adjusts the sunflower pot so it faces the empty balcony across the alley, the one that used to have his plants, and she smiles like she’s talking to both the flower and the air.

shot3: She takes a deep breath, opens her phone, and plays an old voice message of him laughing, then rests the phone beside the sunflower so it can “hear,” and her eyes soften.

shot4: She tapes a tiny handwritten note to the sunflower’s stem that reads “WELCOME HOME,” practicing how she’ll act when he sees it, even though he hasn’t told her what time he’ll arrive.

shot5: She leans on the railing and starts talking to the sunflower out loud — rehearsing casual lines like “Oh hey, you’re back?” and then immediately rejecting them because they sound too calm for how fast her heart is beating.

shot6: A breeze almost knocks the pot, and she grabs it quickly and holds it against her chest, whispering “No no no, you’re the whole plan,” then laughs at how ridiculous and happy she sounds.

shot7: Sunset warms the balcony orange, and she fixes her hair using the reflection in the sunflower’s glazed pot, checks her smile, checks it again, then leaves the pot right on the edge like a signal flare.

shot8: Night settles, lights begin turning on in the windows around her, and Lina sits beside the sunflower with her knees pulled up and her head resting on her arms, waiting with this one bright yellow promise between them and whispering, “I’m here.”
