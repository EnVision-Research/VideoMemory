shot2: In the packed morning subway, he instinctively shields the old watch on his wrist like it’s the only thing that matters.
shot3: In the meeting room, while his boss pressures him about deadlines, he taps the exposed watch face at his cuff to hold himself together.
shot4: At sunset on the office rooftop smoking area, he sits alone, opens the back of the watch, and finds a folded old bus ticket inside.
shot5: At a rundown long-distance bus station at night, gripping the watch, he finally calls his father after a long silence between them.
shot6: Sitting on a plastic bench by the station, he tells his father, “The watch is broken. I don’t know how to fix it,” and it sounds more like asking for help than complaining.
shot7: In a tiny repair shop, the watchmaker says, “Someone’s been taking real care of this watch for years,” and he just listens, eyes softening for the first time.
shot8: The next morning, wearing the repaired watch, he turns off his work notifications and starts typing his resignation letter, as if he’s taking back control of his own time.
